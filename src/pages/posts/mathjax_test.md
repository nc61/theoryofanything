<h2> What we want to show </h2>

To begin this discussion, we state the electromagnetic wave equation for the scalar ($\varphi$) and vector ($\mathbf{A}$) potentials sourced by some time-dependent charge ($\rho$) and current $(\mathbf{J})$ density, respectively. In the Lorenz gauge, the sources and fields are related by the separate wave equations
$$
\begin{align}
    \nabla^2 \varphi - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2 \varphi}{\partial t^2} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0}\rho(\mathbf{r},t) \nonumber \\
    \nabla^2 \mathbf{A} - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{A}}{\partial t^2} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r},t). \label{eq:emg}
\end{align} 
$$
Supposing that we know the electromagnetic potentials at every point in space, Eq\.$\ref{eq:emg}$ allows us to compute the charge and current densities that generated them. In the interest of practicality, our goal here is to invert this expression: we want to be able to compute the electromagnetic potentials generated by a given charge and current density. We will show that these relationships are given by
$$
\begin{align}
    \varphi(\mathbf{r}, t) &= \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0}\int \frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} d\mathbf{r}' \nonumber \\
    \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) &= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} d\mathbf{r}',
\end{align}
$$
which requires a surprisingly lengthy derivation. The symbol $t_r = t - |\mathbf{r} -\mathbf{r}'|/c$ is the retarded time, which indicates that the change in potential due to a disturbance in charge density $\rho$ (or current density) at position $\mathbf{r}'$ is only apparent at a position $\mathbf{r}$ once the field disturbance propagates the intervening distance $|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|$ at the speed of light. 

<h2> The impulse response (Green function) </h2>

The wave equations in Eq.$\ \ref{eq:emg}$ take the general form

$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gf_deL}
    \hat{L}(\mathbf{r},t)f(\mathbf{r},t) = s(\mathbf{r},t),
\end{equation}
$$
<details>
<summary> Linear operators for the electromagnetic potentials </summary>

The exact expressions for the linear operators are

$$
\begin{equation}
    \hat{L}_\varphi(\mathbf{r},t) := \epsilon_0 \left(\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{c^2} \partial_t^2\right)
\end{equation}
$$
which acts on the scalar potential by $\hat{L}_\varphi(\mathbf{r},t) \varphi(\mathbf{r},t) = \rho(\mathbf{r},t)$ and
$$
\begin{equation}
    \hat{L}_A(\mathbf{r},t) := \frac{1}{\mu_0} \left(\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{c^2} \partial_t^2\right)
\end{equation}
$$
which acts on each component of the vector potential $\hat{L}_A(\mathbf{r},t) A_i(\mathbf{r},t) = J_i(\mathbf{r},t)$ for $i = x,y,z$. Since the equations differ at most by a pre-factor, it is easy to swap out this pre-factor to find the other components once one component is solved.

</details>

in which the linear operator $\hat{L}$ acting on some profile $f(\mathbf{r},t)$ (in our case, the potentials) results in a quantity equal to the source term $s(\mathbf{r},t)$ (in our case, the charge and current densities). While the form specialized to the case of electromagnetic potentials is accessible in the box above, we will choose to analyze the general form to emphasize that the process of inverting the linear operator to yield $f$ from $s$ can be done for an arbitrary linear system and that there is nothing special about electromagnetic fields in this sense.

The desired inversion of Eq. $\ref{eq:gf_deL}$ is achieved by computing the Green function, which is just a fancy name for the spatiotemporal impulse response. That is to say that the Green function $G$ is the field $f$ at position $\mathbf{r}$ and time $t$ generated by some instantaneous excitation at position $\mathbf{r}'$ and time $t'$. We denote this function as $G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t')$, indicating that $G$ is directly a function of $\mathbf{r}$ and $t$ while the parameters $\mathbf{r}'$ and $t'$ detail the position and time of the excitation. To be more specific, the instantaneous excitations are modelled by delta functions so that the governing equation that defines the Green function is

$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gf_L}
    \hat{L}(\mathbf{r},t)G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t') = \delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')\delta(t - t').
\end{equation}
$$

We can pause here and ask how Eq.$\ \ref{eq:gf_L}$ is useful to us. The answer lies in the fact that any arbitrary source $s(\mathbf{r},t)$ can be expanded in the delta function basis by

$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gf_delta}
    s(\mathbf{r},t) = \int s(\mathbf{r}',t')\delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')\delta(t - t') d\mathbf{r}' dt'.
\end{equation}
$$

This integral is trivially true, but it is convenient because it allows us to act on each side of Eq.$\ \ref{eq:gf_L}$ by the integral operator $\int d\mathbf{r}' dt' s(\mathbf{r}',t')$ for some arbitrary source distribution $s$. The end result is 

$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gf_LG}
    \hat{L}(\mathbf{r}',t')\int G(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{t};\mathbf{r}',t') \rho(\mathbf{r}',t') d\mathbf{r}'dt' = \rho(\mathbf{r},t).
\end{equation}
$$

The right hand side of Eq. $\ref{eq:gf_LG}$ came from Eq. $\ref{eq:gf_delta}$ and the left hand side was simplified by noting that the linear operator acts on unprimed coordinates so it can be pulled out of the integral. Finally comparing Eq. $\ref{eq:gf_LG}$ to Eq. $\ref{eq:gf_deL}$, we find that we are able to find the field 

$$
\begin{equation}
    \varphi(\mathbf{r},t) = \int G(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{t};\mathbf{r}',t') \rho(\mathbf{r}',t') d\mathbf{r}'dt'
\end{equation}
$$
using the impulse response (Green) function $G$.

<h2> Specialize: The electromagnetic Green function </h2>

Now we just specialize to the case of electromagnetic waves. Things are easier if we choose coordinates where $\mathbf{r}' = 0$, so we are looking to solve the equation
$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Green}
    \left(\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\right)G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t') = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0}\delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')\delta(t - t')
\end{equation}
$$
It is straightforward to solve Eq. $\ref{eq:Green}$ by expanding both sides in the Fourier basis so that the differential operators reduce to momenta $\mathbf{k}$ and energies $\omega$. Doing so, we find that the Green function is
$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Gs}
    G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t') = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^4}\frac{c^2}{\varepsilon_0}\int \frac{1}{\omega^2 - c^2 k^2} e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}') - \omega(t - t'))}d\mathbf{k} d\omega,
\end{equation}
$$
<details>
<summary> Derivation </summary>

We solve with a plane wave expansion of $G$
$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pwe}
    G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t') = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^4}\int \tilde{G}(\mathbf{k},\omega; \mathbf{r}',t') e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega t)}d\mathbf{k} d\omega,
\end{equation}
$$
where $\tilde{G}(\mathbf{k},\omega;\mathbf{r}',t')$ is the $3 + 1$-dimensional Fourier transform defined as
$$
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{G}(\mathbf{k},\omega;\mathbf{r}',t') = \int G(\mathbf{r},t; \mathbf{r}',t') e^{-i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega t)}d\mathbf{k} d\omega.
\end{equation}
$$
Writing $G(\mathbf{r},t)$ using the inverse transform, we have the equation
$$
\begin{equation}
    \left(\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\right)\int \tilde{G}(\mathbf{k},\omega; \mathbf{r}',t') e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega t)}d\mathbf{k}d\omega = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0}\int e^{-i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r}' - \omega t')} e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega t)} d\mathbf{k}d\omega.
\end{equation}
$$
In the transform representation, the operator acts on the plane wave component so that
$$
\begin{equation}
    \int \left[\left(\frac{\omega^2}{c^2} - k^2\right) \tilde{G}(\mathbf{k},\omega; \mathbf{r}',t') - \frac{e^{-i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r}' - \omega t')}}{\epsilon_0} \right]e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega t)}d\mathbf{k}d\omega = 0.
\end{equation}
$$
It follows from the orthonormality of the plane waves that the term in brackets must equal zero, which can then be rearranged for
$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gf_fr}
    \tilde{G}(\mathbf{k},\omega;\mathbf{r}',t') = \frac{c^2}{\epsilon_0}\frac{e^{-i(\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r}' - \omega t')}}{\omega^2 - c^2k^2}
\end{equation}
$$
Finally, use the inverse Fourier transform relation in Eq. $\ref{eq:pwe}$ to compute $G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t')$ from the result in Eq. $\ref{eq:gf_fr}$:
$$
\begin{equation}
    G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t') = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^4}\frac{c^2}{\varepsilon_0}\int \frac{1}{\omega^2 - c^2 k^2} e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}') - \omega(t - t'))}d\mathbf{k} d\omega,
\end{equation}
$$
</details>

which is just the shifted version of $\mathscr{F}^{-1}\left\{G(\mathbf{k},\omega;0,0)\right\}(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}', t-t')$. Consequently, it suffices to calculate the response $G(\mathbf{r},t;0,0)$ and replace $\mathbf{r}$ and $t$ by $\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'$ and $t - t'$, respectively, in the final result. Compute this quantity by performing the integration using spherical coordinates:

$$
\begin{align}\label{eq:G1}
    G(\mathbf{r},t;0,0) &= \frac{2}{(2\pi)^3}\frac{c^2}{\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{ir} \int_{0}^\infty {\color{red} \Omega(k,t)} k \left(e^{ikr} - e^{-ikr}\right) dk \\
    \label{eq:Om}{\color{red}\Omega(k,t) }&=P.V.\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{e^{-i\omega t}}{(\omega - ck)(\omega + ck)}d\omega,
\end{align}
$$
<details>
<summary>Derivation</summary>

Begin with the Green function for a source at the origin at time $t = 0$:
$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Gs_der}
    G(\mathbf{r},t;0,0) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^4}\frac{c^2}{\varepsilon_0}\int \frac{1}{\omega^2 - c^2 k^2} e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} - \omega t)}d\mathbf{k} d\omega.
\end{equation}
$$
Convert Eq. $\ref{eq:Gs_der}$ into spherical coordinates by making the substitution
$$
\begin{align}
k_x &= k\sin\theta \cos\phi \\
k_y &= k\sin\theta \sin\phi \\
k_z &= k\cos\theta.
\end{align}
$$
Note that $G(\mathbf{r},t;0,0)$ is a function which takes in coordinates $\mathbf{r}$ and $t$ and outputs a number. Since we integrate $\mathbf{k}$ over all angles $\theta$ and $\phi$, we may freely choose the $k_z$ direction to be parallel to the $z$ component of coordinate $\mathbf{r}$. Doing so allows us to simplify the dot product formula $\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r} = kr \cos \gamma$, where $\gamma$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{k}$. With this choice of $z$ axis, the angle $\gamma$ is simply the polar angle $\theta$ so that $\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{r} = kr \cos\theta$. With this substitution, we integrate over the spherical volume element $d\mathbf{k} = k^2 \sin^2\theta dk d\theta d\phi$ to find

$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:G0}
    G(\mathbf{r},t;0,0) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^4}\frac{c^2}{\epsilon_0} \int_{0}^\infty dk\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{k^2}{\omega^2 - c^2 k^2} e^{-i\omega t} d\omega \int_0^\pi e^{ikr\cos\theta} \sin \theta d\theta \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi.
\end{equation}
$$
The integral over azimuthal angle $\phi$ in Eq. $\ref{eq:G0}$ is trivial because the integrand lacks $\phi$-dependence. Integration over the polar angle $\theta$ is done by substituting $u = ikr \cos \theta$ and $du = -ikr \sin \theta d\theta$ (and accordingly, $\sin\theta d\theta = - du/ikr$). What remains is the integral over $k$ and $\omega$,
$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:G1_der}
    G(\mathbf{r},t;0,0) = \frac{2}{(2\pi)^3}\frac{c^2}{\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{ir} \int_{0}^\infty k \left(e^{ikr} - e^{-ikr}\right) dk\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{e^{-i\omega t}}{\omega^2 - c^2k^2}  d\omega.
\end{equation}
$$
We will first evaluate the rightmost integral in Eq. $\ref{eq:G1}$ over $\omega$. For convenience, denote this quantity as $\Omega(k,t)$. To begin, factor the denominator as $\omega^2 - c^2k^2 = (\omega - ck)(\omega + ck)$. With this factorization, it is clear that there are singularities in the integrand when $\omega = \pm ck$. Therefore, the best we can do is to compute the principal value of the integral
$$
\begin{equation}
    \Omega(k,t) = P.V.\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{e^{-i\omega t}}{(\omega - ck)(\omega + ck)}d\omega,
\end{equation},
$$
which, as shown in Eq. $\ref{eq:rlim}$, removes an infinitesimally small symmetric interval in the integration limits about each pole. For a hand-wavy argument on why the principal value is satisfactory, first note that $G(\mathbf{r},t;\mathbf{r}',t')$ always exists under an integral sign as a kernel which integrates against a charge or current function to yield a field profile. Thus, the principal value $G$ is well-defined as a distribution. This is not so strange, because we are already working in the space of distributions when we introduce the Dirac delta functions $\delta(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')$ and $\delta(t - t')$. For well-behaved test functions $\rho$ and $\mathbf{J}$, the integral in the neighborhood about each pole is antisymmetric so the positive and negative contributions cancel. So it is reasonable to assume that removing the poles does not have a major effect on the physical interpretation achieved from the principal value integration.

</details>
<h2>Principal value integration in the complex plane</h2>

To find $\Omega(k,t)$, evaluate the integral in Eq. $\ref{eq:Om}$. The prescription for principal value integration is to remove a symmetric interval of length $2\epsilon$ about the poles at $\pm ck$. In the end, we take the limit as $\varepsilon \rightarrow 0$ so that the length of the excluded interval reduces to zero:
$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rlim}
	\Omega(k,t) = \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow 0}\left(\int_{-\infty}^{-ck - \epsilon}d\omega + \int_{-ck + \epsilon}^{ck - \epsilon}d\omega + \int_{ck + \epsilon}^{\infty} d\omega \right) \frac{e^{-i\omega t}}{(\omega - ck)(\omega + ck)}.
\end{equation}
$$
While this is an integral over the real line, we can calculate it by integrating over a closed contour in the complex plane and taking advantage of the residue theorem. 

$$
\begin{equation}
	\Omega(k,t) = - \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow 0} \left(\int_{S(-ck,\epsilon)} dz + \int_{S(ck,\epsilon)} dz + \int_{S(0,R)} dz\right)f(z).
\end{equation}
$$

<details>
<summary> Derivation </summary>

To do so, replace the real $\omega$ with complex $z$ so that the integrand becomes
$$
\begin{align}
	f(z) = \frac{e^{-iz t}}{(z - ck)(z + ck)}dz.
\end{align}
$$
We integrate over a closed closed contour $\mathscr{C}$ that contains the limits of the principal value integration along the real line by defining
$$
\begin{align}\label{eq:path}
	&\oint_{\mathcal{C}} f(z) dz = \Omega(k,t) + \lim_{R\rightarrow \infty} \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow 0} \left(\int_{S(-ck,\epsilon)} dz + \int_{S(ck,\epsilon)} dz + \int_{S(0,R)} dz\right)f(z).
\end{align}
$$
Though the ordering is swapped, the path traverses the real line from $-\infty$ to $-ck -\varepsilon$, a small semicircle about the pole at $-ck$ ($S(-ck,\varepsilon)$), a path along the real line from $-ck + \varepsilon$ to $ck - \varepsilon$, a small semicircle about the pole at $ck$ ($S(ck,\varepsilon)$), the real line from $ck + \varepsilon$ to $\infty$, and a semicircular arc with infinite radius connecting from $+\infty$ to $-\infty$ ($S(R,\varepsilon)$).

If we define the excursions around each singularity so that $f(z)$ contains no poles within its boundaries, then $f(z)$ is analytic everywhere within the contour so the integral $\oint_{\mathcal{C}} f(z) dz = 0$ by the residue theorem. Eq. $\ref{eq:path}$ then rearranges to
$$
\begin{equation}
	\Omega(k,t) = - \lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow 0} \left(\int_{S(-ck,\epsilon)} dz + \int_{S(ck,\epsilon)} dz + \int_{S(0,R)} dz\right)f(z).
\end{equation}
$$
That is, we can calculate the desired integral $\Omega(k,t)$ by evaluating only the semicircular contours about each pole along with the infinite radius semicircle that connects the positive and negative real axes. Note that choosing contours that exclude poles from the integration region is the most convenient choice, but it is not required. We would find the same end result for $\Omega(k,t)$ if we included the poles inside the contour and accounted for the fact that the residue theorem must be applied with poles inside the integration region.
</details>

Next, we need to choose whether to close the contour in the upper or lower half plane. We are constrained by the requirement that the integral over the infinite semicircular is well defined, and that depends on the sign of the parameter $t$.

The contour must be closed in the lower half plane
<details>
<summary> Derivation </summary>

The constraints on the integration path arise from integration along the arc of radius $R$ over an angle $\pi$. To determine whether this arc should traverse through the upper- or lower-half plane depend on the asymptotic behavior of the numerator $f(z) = e^{-izt}$. Parametrizing in polar coordinates, the function along the arc is
$$
\begin{equation}
	f(z) = e^{-iR(\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)t} =  e^{-iR\cos \theta t}  e^{R \sin \theta t}.
\end{equation}
$$
The condition $t > 0$ requires the value of $\sin\theta$ to be negative, since positive values of $\sin\theta$ would cause the term $e^{R \sin \theta t}$ to diverge as $R \rightarrow \infty$. Consequently, we must choose a contour that closes in the lower half plane for which $e^{R \sin \theta t} \rightarrow 0$ as $R\rightarrow \infty$. 
$$
\begin{equation}
	\left|\int_{S(0,R)} e^{- i R t \cos \theta} e^{-R t \sin \theta} dz \right| \leq \int \left| R e^{-R t}\right||d\theta| \leq \pi \left|R e^{-Rt}\right|
\end{equation}
$$
Taking the limit as $R\rightarrow \infty$, we find that the exponential dominates so that $\lim_{R\rightarrow \infty} |Re^{-Rt}| = 0$. 
</details>

$$
\begin{align}
    \int_{S(-ck,\epsilon)}f(z) dz &= -\frac{i\pi e^{ickt}}{2ck} \\
	\int_{S^+(ck,\epsilon)}f(z) dz &= \frac{i\pi e^{-ickt}}{2ck}
\end{align}
$$

<summary> Derivation </summary>

As a result, we must close the contour by a semicircle in the lower half plane. In this case, the semicircular paths around the poles must also extend into the lower half plane so that the contour contains no singularities. Around the pole at $-ck$, we define $z' = z + ck$ so that $dz' = dz$ and
$$
\begin{equation}
	\int_{S(-ck,\epsilon)}f(z) dz = e^{ickt} \int_{S^+(0,\epsilon)} \left(\frac{e^{-i z' t}}{z' - 2ck}\right)\frac{1}{z'}dz'
\end{equation}
$$
The expression in parentheses is analytic on a disk of radius $2ck$ centered at $z' = 0$. Since the integral takes place along a semicirucle with radius $r < 2ck$, the analyticity condition holds for the integration path so we can expand as a power series. 
$$
\begin{equation}
	u(z) = \left(a_0 + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}a_n z^n\right)\frac{1}{z} = \frac{a_0}{z} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}a_n z^{n - 1} = \frac{a_0}{z} + P(z)
\end{equation}
$$
where we collected the power series into the function $P(z)$. Since $P(z)$ is a power series, it is bounded by some maximum value $M$ along the semicircular contour. The integral over this term is then bounded by
$$
\begin{equation}
	\left|\int_{S(0,\epsilon)}P(z)dz\right| \leq \int_{S(0,\epsilon)}M |dz| =  M \pi\epsilon.
\end{equation}
$$
Since $\lim_{\epsilon \rightarrow 0} M\pi \epsilon = 0$, the same applies to the contour integral over $P(z)$. What remains is the integral over the singular term
$$
\begin{equation}
	\int_{S(-ck,\epsilon)}f(z) dz = -\frac{e^{ickt}}{2ck}\int_{S^+(\epsilon,0)} \frac{dz}{z} = -\frac{i e^{ickt}}{2ck}\int_{-\pi}^0 d\theta = -\frac{i\pi e^{ickt}}{2ck}
\end{equation}
$$
Since the contour is a semicircle of radius $\epsilon$, we define $z = \epsilon e^{i\theta}$ so that $dz = i\epsilon e^{i\theta} d\theta$.

The integral over the other pole goes exactly the same to find
$$
\begin{equation}
	\int_{S^+(ck,\epsilon)}f(z) dz = \frac{i\pi e^{-ickt}}{2ck}
\end{equation}
$$
